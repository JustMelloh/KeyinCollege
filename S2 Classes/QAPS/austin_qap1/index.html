The Common Raven


What is Corvus corax? (Common Raven)

The Corvus corax is a large passerine bird species of the family Corvidae.
It is the most widely distributed of all corvids, found across the northern hemisphere. It is a raven known by many names at the subspecies level; there are atleast eight subspecies with little variation in appearance.


Eight Subspecies

  • Northern Raven (C. c. principalis)
  • Western Raven (C. c. sinuatus)
  • North Atlantic (C. c. varius)
  • North African Raven (C. c. tingitanus)
  • North Eurasian Raven (C. c. corax)
  • South Eurasian Raven (C. c. subcorax)
  • Tibetan Raven (C. c tibetans)
  • Kamchatkan Raven (C.c. Kamstschaticus)

Interesting facts

  • The Common Raven is an acrobatic flier, often doing rolls and somersaults in the air. Young birds are fond of playing games with sticks, repeatedly dropping them, then diving to catch them in midair.
  • Ravens are smart, which makes them dangerous predators. They often work in pairs to raid seabird colonies, with one bird distracting an incubating adult and the other waiting to grab an egg or chick as soon as its uncovered.
  • People the world over sense a certain kind of personality in ravens. Edgar Allan Poe clearly found them a little creepy. The captive ravens at the Tower of London are beloved and perhaps a little feared: legend has it that if they ever leave the tower, the British Empire will crumble. Native people of the Pacific Northwest regard the raven as an incurable trickster, bringing fire to people by stealing it from the sun, and stealing salmon only to drop them in rivers all over the world.

What about its habitat?


The common raven can thrive in varied climates; this species has the largest range of any member of their gens and one of the largest of all passerine. They can range throughout the Holarctic from Arctic and temperate habitats in North America and Eurasia. They are more common in Scotland, Wales and northern England.

Most common ravens prefer wooded areas with large expanses of open land, or coastal regions for its nesting and feeding grounds. In some areas with dense population, they take advantage of a plentiful food supply and have an increased surge in numbers.

Are you smarter than a common raven?


The brain of the common raven is among the largest of any bird species. Their hyperpallium is large for a bird, and display an ability in problem-solving along with other cognitive processes such as imitation and insight.

Linguist Derek Bickerton, building on the work of biologist Bernd Heinrich, has debated that ravens are one of only four animals (others being bees, ants and humans) who have demonstrated displacement, the capacity to communicate about objects and events that are distant in space or time.

An experiment designed to evaluate insight and problem-solving involved a piece of meat attached to a string hanging from a perch. In order for the bird to reach the food, it needed to stand on the perch, pull p the string a little at a time and step on the loops to gradually shorten the string. Four of five ravens succeeded. The transition from no success to constant reliable success occured with no demonstrable trial and error learning.
